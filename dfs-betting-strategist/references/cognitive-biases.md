# Cognitive Biases

## The Internal Enemy

As algorithms solve the mathematical aspects of decision-making, the remaining edge is **Behavioral**. Human psychology systematically deviates from rationality, creating exploitable patterns in competitive environments.

## Confirmation Bias

### Definition and Impact
**Confirmation Bias**: Seeking data that supports your "sleeper" pick while ignoring contradictory evidence.

### Real-World Example
- **DFS Context**: Focusing only on stats that support your favorite player while ignoring red flags
- **Investment Context**: Only reading research that confirms your existing positions
- **Career Context**: Seeking information that validates your current job choice

### Professional Solution
**Actively try to disprove your thesis** through:
- **Falsification**: Attempt to prove your hypothesis wrong
- **Devil's Advocate**: Force yourself to argue the opposite position
- **Disconfirming Evidence**: Actively seek contradictory data

### Implementation Framework
1. **Initial Thesis**: "Player X will outperform"
2. **Falsification Test**: "What would prove Player X will underperform?"
3. **Data Collection**: Gather evidence for both scenarios
4. **Decision**: Make choice only after considering both sides

## Recency Bias

### Definition and Impact
**Recency Bias**: Overweighting the most recent events and underweighting historical data.

### DFS Context
- **Overweighting last game**: A player had a great performance last week, so you assume they'll repeat it
- **Ignoring sample size**: Making decisions based on 1-2 games rather than season-long patterns
- **Hot hand fallacy**: Believing players are "due" for good performance after bad games

### Professional Solution
**Trust the long-term baseline** over recent outliers:
- **Sample Size**: Require sufficient historical data
- **Regression Analysis**: Expect outliers to move toward mean
- **Process Focus**: Evaluate decision quality, not recent outcomes

### Mathematical Application
```
Weighted Average = (Recent Games × Weight) + (Season Average × (1 - Weight))
Weight increases with sample size
```

## Loss Aversion

### Definition and Impact
**Loss Aversion**: Humans feel the pain of loss approximately **2x more** than the joy of gain.

### Behavioral Manifestations
- **Risk-seeking in losses**: Taking excessive risk to avoid losses
- **Risk-averse in gains**: Taking too little risk when ahead
- **Status quo bias**: Preferring current state over beneficial changes

### DFS Examples
- **Playing "not to lose"**: Choosing safe players in tournaments when you need upside
- **Holding losers too long**: Refusing to pivot from poor-performing players
- **Early profit taking**: Selling winners too quickly to lock in gains

### Professional Solution
**Reframe entry fees/investments as "sunk costs"**:
- **Immediate Write-off**: Consider money spent as already gone
- **Forward-Looking**: Evaluate only current position, not past decisions
- **Expected Value Focus**: Make decisions based on future outcomes, not past investments

### Implementation
```
Before: "I've already spent $100, I can't afford to lose more"
After: "I have $100 at risk right now. What's the optimal decision?"
```

## The Dunning-Kruger Effect

### Definition and Impact
**Amateurs overestimate their skill** and underestimate variance. They attribute wins to genius and losses to "bad beats."

### Characteristic Patterns
- **Overconfidence**: Believing you have special insight
- **Attribution errors**: Success = skill, failure = luck
- **Learning plateau**: Stop improving after initial success

### The Professional Mindset
**Radical Accountability**:
- **Attributes wins to variance**: Humility in success
- **Attributes losses to process errors**: Learning from mistakes
- **Continuous improvement**: Treat every decision as a learning opportunity

### Feedback Loop Framework
1. **Decision Made**: Enter position based on analysis
2. **Outcome Occurs**: Record actual result
3. **Process Evaluation**: Was decision-making sound?
4. **Learning Extraction**: What can be improved?
5. **System Update**: Adjust methods based on learning

## Pattern Recognition Biases

### Apophenia
**Definition**: Seeing patterns in random data that don't actually exist.

### Hot Hand Fallacy
**Belief**: After a series of successes, success is more likely.
**Reality**: Each event is independent unless there's causal connection.

### Gambler's Fallacy
**Belief**: After a series of losses, a win is "due."
**Reality**: Past events don't influence future probabilities.

### Professional Approach
- **Statistical significance**: Require sufficient sample sizes
- **Causal analysis**: Understand why patterns exist
- **Independence testing**: Verify events are truly independent

## Overconfidence Bias

### Manifestations
- **Precision overconfidence**: Believing forecasts are more accurate than they are
- **Calibration errors**: Not accounting for uncertainty ranges
- **Planning fallacy**: Underestimating time and resource requirements

### Professional Mitigation
- **Confidence intervals**: Always provide uncertainty ranges
- **Historical validation**: Test predictions against past performance
- **Bayesian updating**: Adjust confidence based on new evidence

## Anchoring Bias

### Definition
**Anchoring Bias**: Over-relying on the first piece of information received.

### DFS Examples
- **Initial price印象**: Being overly influenced by a player's draft position
- **First projection**: Giving too much weight to initial lineup projections
- **Market sentiment**: Getting stuck on popular narratives

### Professional Approach
- **Multiple anchors**: Consider various reference points
- **Objective valuation**: Focus on intrinsic value, not market prices
- **Dynamic adjustment**: Update valuations as new information arrives

## Social Proof Bias

### Definition
**Social Proof**: Following the crowd because "everyone else is doing it."

### Market Applications
- **Following popular picks**: "Everyone is roster this player"
- **Consensus trades**: Joining crowded positions
- **FOMO investing**: Buying into bubbles

### Contrarian Strategy
- **Independent analysis**: Make decisions based on data, not crowd behavior
- **Ownership tracking**: Monitor what percentage of the field holds each position
- **Value identification**: Find opportunities others are missing

## Sunk Cost Fallacy

### Definition
**Sunk Cost**: Continuing a behavior because of previously invested resources (time, money, effort).

### Examples
- **Failing business**: Continuing because "I've already invested so much"
- **Bad relationship**: Staying because of time invested
- **Losing position**: Not selling because of purchase price

### Professional Response
- **Sunk cost ignore**: Only consider future costs and benefits
- **Opportunity cost**: What else could you do with those resources?
- **Real-time optimization**: Continuously evaluate current position

---

*Understanding cognitive biases is not about eliminating them entirely—it's about recognizing when they are affecting your decisions and having systems to mitigate their impact.*
