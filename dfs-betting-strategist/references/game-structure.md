# Game Structure

## The Fundamental Theorem of DFS

The core principle of strategic decision-making in uncertain environments is captured in the Fundamental Theorem:

> **Would you have made the same decision if you knew the outcomes of what your opponents did?**

This aligns with the Fundamental Theorem of Poker: "Every time you play a hand differently from the way you would have played it if you could see all your opponents' cards, they gain; and every time you play your hand the same way you would have played it if you could see all their cards, they lose."

## Information Asymmetry as the Edge

### The Data Revolution Paradox
In the information age, raw data is commoditized. Everyone has the same stats. The new Information Asymmetry is not access to data, but the **processing** of data.

### Real-World Applications

#### The Lemon Problem (Akerlof's Market)
Akerlof's market for lemons illustrates how asymmetry destroys value. In used car markets, buyers assume cars are defective because sellers know more. The market collapses due to information imbalance.

**Business Application**: In salary negotiations, the employer holds the information asymmetry (budget knowledge). The candidate reduces this asymmetry through market research (Glassdoor, networking), effectively "seeing the opponent's cards" to play the hand optimally.

#### Salary Inefficiency as Arbitrage
In DFS, Expected Value is generated by identifying "Salary Inefficiency." Player salaries are static (set days in advance), but information is dynamic. If a starter is injured and a minimum-priced backup takes their role, the backup's projection skyrockets while their price remains low. This gap is Arbitrage.

**Market Application**: In 2008, real estate prices collapsed. The "intrinsic value" of the homes (rental yield) remained high, but the "salary" (market price) plummeted due to forced selling. Investors who bought in 2009 were exploiting a massive salary inefficiency. They bought $1 of cash flow for $0.50.

## The OODA Loop and Sequential Dynamics

### Colonel John Boyd's Framework
Colonel John Boyd's OODA Loop (Observe, Orient, Decide, Act) provides the tactical framework for navigating the transition from imperfect to perfect information.

### Game Types and Information Flow

#### Simultaneous Games
In standard DFS contests, players lock lineups simultaneously. Everyone acts on the same imperfect information (projections) without knowing their opponents' moves. This mirrors a sealed-bid auction.

#### Sequential Games (Late Swap)
DFS platforms that allow "Late Swap" (changing players after the first games have started) transform the game into a sequential one. As real-world events occur (a player is injured, a game goes into overtime), imperfect information becomes perfect.

**The Professional's Process**:
1. **Observe** - Watch real-world events unfold
2. **Orient** - Reassess lineup relative to field
3. **Decide** - Determine new optimal path
4. **Act** - Execute player swaps

### Corporate Applications

#### Real Options Theory
In business strategy, the ability to delay a decision until imperfect information becomes perfect is valuable. This is quantified in finance as **Real Options Theory**—the value of the right, but not the obligation, to undertake a business initiative.

**Examples**:
- **The Option to Defer**: A mining company holding a lease on land but waiting to drill until gold prices rise
- **The Premium for Flexibility**: Companies paying more for leasing vs. buying, contractors vs. employees

#### Late Swap Strategy in Business
The "Late Swap" strategy in business—pivoting product features or marketing spend in real-time based on user feedback—is an arbitrage against competitors who are rigid. The company that cycles through the OODA Loop fastest—turning observation into action—gains a compounding advantage.

## The Jelly Bean Principle

### Comparative Expected Value
The text uses a jar of jelly beans to teach comparative EV. If you are offered a bet on:
- Red beans (40% prob) at 2:1 odds, or  
- Yellow beans (10% prob) at 20:1 odds

You must bet Yellow. Even though Red is more likely to win, Yellow has the higher EV:
- Red: 0.40 × $2.00 = $0.80 EV
- Yellow: 0.10 × $20.00 = $2.00 EV

### Venture Capital Parallel
This explains why Venture Capital funds bet on risky startups (Yellow Beans) rather than safe bonds (Red Beans). One 100x winner pays for 99 losers. The EV is in the magnitude of the win, not the frequency.
