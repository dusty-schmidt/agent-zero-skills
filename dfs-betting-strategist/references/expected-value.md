# Expected Value Theory

## The Engine of Rationality

Expected Value (EV) is the "Holy Grail" of decision-making. It is the weighted average of all possible outcomes, providing the mathematical foundation for rational choice in uncertain environments.

## The Fundamental Formula

```
EV = Σ(Pi × Vi)
```

Where:
- **Pi** = Probability of outcome i
- **Vi** = Value (payoff) of outcome i

## The Psychology of EV: Process Over Results

### The Most Difficult Mental Leap
The most challenging aspect for novices is divorcing the quality of a decision from its result.

### Scenario Analysis
**Scenario**: You wager $100 on a 90% chance to win $1,000. You lose (the 10% outcome occurs).

**Amateur Response**: "I was stupid to make that bet."
**Professional Response**: "I paid $100 for a lottery ticket worth $900 ($1000 × 0.9). I made $800 in Expected Value instantly. The fact that I lost cash is irrelevant variance."

### Core Principle
**Process > Result**. The quality of a decision is determined at the time it's made, not by the outcome.

## Insurance Underwriting as EV Machine

### How Insurance Works
Insurance companies are EV machines. They do not know if your house will burn down. They know that in a pool of 100,000 houses, 50 will burn down. They price premiums such that:

```
∑ Premiums > ∑ Claims
```

They essentially monetize the law of large numbers.

### Personal Finance Application
When you buy insurance, you are accepting a slightly negative EV wager (paying more than statistical risk) to purchase protection against **Absorbing Barriers** (Ruin).

**Rationality**: Take negative EV bets only when the cost of losing is existential (bankruptcy/death).

## Salary Inefficiency as Arbitrage

### DFS Application
EV is generated by identifying "Salary Inefficiency":
- Player salaries are static (set days in advance)
- Information is dynamic
- When a starter is injured, a minimum-priced backup's projection skyrockets while price remains low
- This gap is pure arbitrage

### Market Applications

#### The 2008 Housing Example
- **Intrinsic value**: Rental yield remained high
- **Market price**: Collapsed due to forced selling
- **Investor advantage**: Bought $1 of cash flow for $0.50 in 2009
- **Result**: Massive salary inefficiency exploitation

#### Corporate Applications
- **Distressed asset buying**: Acquiring undervalued companies during market panic
- **Talent acquisition**: Hiring quality employees during economic downturns
- **Real estate**: Buying during recessions when prices are temporarily depressed

## The Jelly Bean Principle

### Comparative Expected Value
Using jelly beans to teach comparative EV:

**Bet Options**:
- Red beans: 40% probability at 2:1 odds
- Yellow beans: 10% probability at 20:1 odds

**EV Calculations**:
- Red: 0.40 × $2.00 = $0.80 EV
- Yellow: 0.10 × $20.00 = $2.00 EV

**Decision**: Bet Yellow, despite lower probability, because higher EV.

### Venture Capital Parallel
This explains why VC funds bet on risky startups (Yellow Beans) rather than safe bonds (Red Beans):
- **One 100x winner** pays for 99 losers
- **EV is in the magnitude** of the win, not the frequency
- **Portfolio approach**: Make many small bets on high-EV outcomes

## EV in Career Decisions

### Job Change Analysis
**Current Job**:
- 80% chance of 10% raise next year = $8,000 expected value
- 20% chance of layoff = -$50,000 loss

**New Job Offer**:
- 60% chance of 30% raise = $18,000 expected value
- 40% chance of failure = -$20,000 loss

**EV Calculation**:
- Current: (0.80 × $8,000) + (0.20 × -$50,000) = $6,400 - $10,000 = -$3,600
- New: (0.60 × $18,000) + (0.40 × -$20,000) = $10,800 - $8,000 = $2,800

**Decision**: Take new job based on higher EV.

### Skill Investment
**Option 1**: Learn Python ($2,000 cost, 70% chance of $10,000 salary increase)
- EV: (0.70 × $10,000) - $2,000 = $5,000

**Option 2**: Learn Machine Learning ($5,000 cost, 30% chance of $50,000 salary increase)
- EV: (0.30 × $50,000) - $5,000 = $10,000

**Decision**: Machine Learning based on higher EV.

## Advanced EV Concepts

### Dynamic EV
EV changes as new information arrives:
- **Pre-game**: Player projected for 15 points
- **Injury news**: Projection drops to 8 points
- **Weather update**: Projection increases to 18 points
- **Late scratch**: Player ruled out (0 points)

### Conditional EV
EV calculations based on specific conditions:
- **Stacked offense**: QB and WR both have higher EV together
- **Game script dependent**: Running backs in blowout games
- **Opponent specific**: Against weak defenses

### Portfolio EV
Multiple correlated decisions:
- **Diversified approach**: Multiple small EV advantages
- **Concentrated bets**: Few large EV advantages
- **Correlation adjustment**: Reduce double-counting of risks

## EV and Risk Management

### Kelly Criterion Integration
EV alone doesn't determine optimal bet size. Combine with Kelly Criterion for bankroll allocation.

### Risk of Ruin Considerations
- **High EV, high variance**: May still lead to ruin if bet too large
- **Lower EV, lower variance**: More sustainable long-term approach
- **Optimal approach**: Balance EV maximization with survival probability

### Liquidity Management
- **Cash flow needs**: Don't tie up all capital in long-term EV bets
- **Opportunity costs**: Consider alternative uses of capital
- **Time horizon**: Match investment timeline to liquidity needs

---

*Expected Value is not about predicting the future—it's about making decisions that are profitable on average, even when individual outcomes vary.*
