# Probabilistic Forecasting

## Escaping Determinism

Humans are biologically wired for determinism. We crave "locks"—guaranteed outcomes. The professional re-wires their brain for **Probabilistic Forecasting**, viewing every potential future as a distribution of outcomes rather than single-point predictions.

## The Bell Curve: Median vs. Range

### Understanding Distributions
When a projection system predicts an athlete will score "15 points," it is not a prediction; it is the **median** of a distribution.

### The Empirical Rule (Normal Distribution)
In a normal distribution:
- **68%** of outcomes fall within one standard deviation (σ) of the mean (μ)
- **95%** within two (2σ)
- **99.7%** within three (3σ)

### Standard Deviation (σ) Application
**σ measures the volatility of the asset.**

**Player A (Low σ)**: Mean 15, Range 12-18 (Reliable, High Floor)
**Player B (High σ)**: Mean 15, Range 0-40 (Volatile, High Ceiling)

## The Barbell Strategy

### Nassim Taleb's Framework
Nassim Taleb's Barbell Strategy suggests avoiding the "middle" risk in favor of combining extreme safety with extreme risk-taking.

### DFS Applications

#### Cash Games (Double Ups)
**Goal**: Merely to not fail (top 50%)
**Optimal Choice**: Player A (Low σ)
**Rationale**: The cost of Player B's variance (risk of scoring 0) outweighs the benefit of his ceiling (40 points), because 40 points pays the same as 18 points.

#### GPP Tournaments
**Goal**: The 99th percentile (winning the lottery)
**Optimal Choice**: Player B (High σ)
**Rationale**: Player A is "dead money." He cannot score 40. Player B is the only rational choice. You accept the risk of 0 for the access to 40.

### Career Applications
A "middle-class" career (mid-level management) often has:
- **Limited upside** (capped salary)
- **Hidden tail risks** (layoffs, inflation)

A "Barbell" career combines:
- **Extreme safety**: Tenured position or stable bond portfolio
- **Extreme risk-taking**: Angel investing, writing a book

This structure limits downside while exposing the individual to positive Black Swans.

## Regression to the Mean vs. The Gambler's Fallacy

### Critical Distinction
Professionals distinguish between independent and dependent events.

### Gambler's Fallacy
**Definition**: Believing a coin must come up Tails because it has hit Heads 10 times.
**Reality**: The events are independent; the probability remains 50%.

### Regression to the Mean
**Definition**: Predicting that an outlier performance will be followed by a performance closer to the average.
**Mechanism**: This is not because the universe owes a correction, but because extreme events are statistically rare.

**Example**: If a salesperson closes 500% of their quota in Q1, betting on them to repeat that in Q2 is a bad bet. They will likely "regress" to their skill baseline.

## Scientific Applications

### The Sophomore Slump
In medicine and sports, the "Sophomore Slump" or "Placebo Effect" often boils down to regression:
1. A patient seeks treatment when their pain is at its peak (outlier event)
2. Regardless of intervention (pill or placebo), pain is statistically likely to regress to mean (subside)
3. Mistaking this regression for causation wastes billions in medical spend and corporate consulting

### Sample Size and Statistical Power
- **Small samples**: High variance, unreliable conclusions
- **Large samples**: Regression to mean, reliable patterns
- **Professional approach**: Trust long-term baselines over recent outliers

## Probability Distributions in Practice

### Normal Distribution (Bell Curve)
**Use Case**: Most athletic performance, market returns
**Characteristics**: Symmetric, predictable ranges
**Application**: Standard player projections, typical market scenarios

### Power Law Distribution (Fat Tails)
**Use Case**: Extreme events, breakthrough performances
**Characteristics**: Small probability of massive outcomes
**Application**: GPP lineups, venture capital, innovation

### Log-Normal Distribution
**Use Case**: Growth processes, compounding effects
**Characteristics**: Cannot go negative, skewed right
**Application**: Salary growth, portfolio returns

## Forecasting Framework

### Multi-Scenario Planning
1. **Base Case** (50% probability): Most likely outcome
2. **Optimistic Case** (25% probability): Better than expected
3. **Pessimistic Case** (25% probability): Worse than expected

### Decision Matrix
| Scenario | Probability | Outcome | Weighted Value |
|----------|-------------|---------|---------------|
| Base | 50% | $100 | $50 |
| Optimistic | 25% | $200 | $50 |
| Pessimistic | 25% | $0 | $0 |
| **Total EV** | | | **$100** |

### Range-Based Projections
Instead of point estimates, use ranges:
- **Conservative Range**: 80% confidence interval
- **Aggressive Range**: 20% confidence interval for upside
- **Tail Risk**: 5% probability scenarios
